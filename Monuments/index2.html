Roman colleseum

The Roman colesseum The Colosseum, built in Rome between 72 and 80 AD, was commissioned by Emperor Vespasian and originally named the Flavian Amphitheater. It served as a venue for gladiatorial combats, animal hunts, and even mock naval battles, offering entertainment to the Roman populace. The Colosseum's construction symbolized the power and benevolence of the Flavian emperors.

Machu pichu peru

Machu Picchu, an Incan citadel, was built in the mid-15th century and abandoned a century later, likely due to the Spanish conquest or a civil war. It's believed to have been constructed as a palatial complex for the Inca emperor Pachacuti. The site was rediscovered in 1911 by Hiram Bingham and was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983.

Stonehege

Stonehenge, a renowned prehistoric monument, evolved over many centuries, beginning around 3100 BC with an earthwork enclosure, and culminating in the iconic stone circle around 2500 BC. It was constructed in stages, with the first phase involving a ditch and bank, followed by the addition of wooden posts and later, the iconic sarsen and bluestone structures. The site was also used as a cremation cemetery for several hundred years.

The great wall of china

The Great Wall of China, a series of fortifications, was built over centuries to protect the country's northern border from nomadic invaders and external threats. It began as a collection of smaller walls in the 7th century BCE and was later connected and expanded by successive dynasties, culminating in the Ming Dynasty's extensive fortifications. The primary purpose of the Great Wall was military defense, guarding against invasions from groups like the Xiongnu and Mongol
